The Justin Welsh Operating System — distilled

Trait 1 — Backstory (be relatable, not untouchable)

Your audience joins your journey when they see themselves in you. Every great backstory has 7 beats:

  • The obstacle (relatable) → internal struggle → external struggle → change event → the spark → the guide → the result.
Justin Welsh's viral backstory post — 14,200 reactions, 828 commentsI got FIRED 3 times before I was 28. I NEVER hit quota in my first 4 sales jobs. At 28, I took a job as one of the first salespeople at a tech company in NYC. I moved to NYC and was paid $40k as I turned 29. I slept on my friend Mara's couch for 5 months. ...The intersection of finding a product, team, culture and city I freaking LOVED. I worked NON-STOP. I won every award possible. I got promoted 5 times. By 33 I became an Executive. I look back and cannot remember the old me.

Your version: selling Quokka Brew dorm-to-dorm → customers asking who made your merch → Quokka Prints → outgrowing it → GetMerch. The obstacle/anxiety/"failed businesses" beats are your gold.

Trait 2 — Polarization (suss out your strong opinions)

Boring = no engagement. Write things people will either strongly agree or strongly disagree with. Nobody is indifferent. Use absolutes ("never," "dead," "always") to stop the scroll.

Example — the "Resumes are dead" postResumes are dead. Your online presence is your new resume. It doesn't matter whether you believe it or not. It's the truth. And what you produce online is the ultimate look into who you are... An online presence requires daily work. It's tough to fake that. A resume? An hour. Anyone can do that.
Example — create vs. consume — 3,851 reactions · 421 comments · 317,298 viewsStop bragging to everyone that you read 25 books a year. Publish one. Stop telling everyone about the podcasts you listen to. Record one. Stop spouting off everything you read in blogs. Write one. There's no reward for the most consumption. Creation? That's a different story.

~80% will love it, ~20% will push back. That's the point — the 20% were never your community.

Trait 3 — Stories (turn lessons into short stories)

People don't remember facts. They remember stories. Take a common lesson in your niche and dramatize it with a real moment.

The legendary "$130 treadmill" story"I paid a guy $130 to move my treadmill in 30 minutes." Told me he had 8 moves that day. Do the math. Have you ever made $1,040 in one day? Everyone wants entrepreneurship to be fancy. Sometimes it's just moving treadmills.

Your versions: "I spent an hour sourcing an inflatable flamingo," "a festival ordered 5,000 shirts in 48 hours," "the customer email that said 'I don't want five vendors, I want one button.'"

Copywriting — Stop. Read. Participate.

  • The Trailer (first 3 lines): break the scroll. The reader commits or bails after line 3 (before "see more"). Put a space between lines.
  • The Meat: teach, show, or suggest the actual value.
  • The Summary / question: end with a question that's easy to answer so people comment.

2026 LinkedIn Algorithm — what actually moves reach

Dwell time = #1 signal Comments = 15× a like Comments 15+ words = 2.5× weight Sweet spot 1,000–1,300 chars

  • The first 3 lines must earn the "see more" click — without it, dwell time never accumulates.
  • LinkedIn tests each post on 2–5% of your network first. Strong early engagement (first 60 min) decides wider reach. Only ~5% of slow-starting posts ever recover.
  • Reply to every comment in the first hour — it doubles comment weight and signals quality. Tell your audience when you post so they show up.
  • A link in the post body drops reach ~60%. Put links in the first comment instead.
  • Document carousels (PDF) and short vertical video (30–90s, captioned) get the biggest format boosts right now.
  • "Great post!" comments are now treated as noise — encourage real replies with a real question.
  • Your headline / About / Experience affect post reach — which is exactly why we're adding GetMerch to your profile.
